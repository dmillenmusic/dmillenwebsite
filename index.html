DALE MILLEN MUSIC

DALE MILLEN studied electronic music composition with Vladimir Ussachevsky at Columbia University. He was guest composer/researcher at Stanford University's CCRMA in 1994 where he composed "Piano Fantasy" using the RTMix, Common Music and Common Lisp Music programs. His composition, "Infinite Vista", was presented at the 1996 International Computer Music Conference in Hong Kong. He has written computer music applications which use fractals and cellular automata to generate music and images ("Fractal Music" and "Cellular Automata Music") and has composed music for musical theater including "Music for Under Milk Wood", "The Bacchae", and "The Caucasian Chalk Circle".

DALE MILLEN - Computer Music Applications

CELLULAR AUTOMATA MUSIC

A Computer Music Application for
Macintosh Computer
macOS

My original Cellular Automata Music application was developed 1988-1990 and was used in presenting a paper at the International Computer Music Conference, 1990, Glasgow. This paper is cited on page 865 of The Computer Music Tutorial by Curtis Roads, MIT Press, 1996.

The original Cellular Automata Music was written in the Pascal programming language and used MIDI Pascal for MIDI programming.

A new version of CELLULAR AUTOMATA MUSIC (2003-2004) is written in Cocoa, Apple object-oriented language, Objective-C, and in the C programming language. The visual presentation in the application is important and the user interface is designed to allow interaction with or response to the cellular automata images while the application is running. Most user control options are located on the main window and can be immediately accessed by the user. Options not required for interactive operation are located in the menu. The size of the application main window cannot be changed and it is recommended that the display resolution be set to 1024 X 768. Cellular Automata Music requires the macOS operating system, a MIDI interface, and a MIDI synthesizer with 16 channels if an external synthesizer is used (the macOS internal synth is the default synthesizer), a display with a 1024 X 768 resolution option, and a computer keyboard with a mouse. Please read the Cellular Automata Music Help menu for application guidelines.

Design goals for this version of Cellular Automata Music included implementation of the following features:

  • an improvisational interactive run mode where the user can change control parameters based on visual observation of the evolving cellular automaton displayed on the computer screen as the application is running.
  • methods to record and save to file all control parameter changes made by the user during a run. It is suggested that files be named using .cam as a suffix. Files are saved in the Documents folder.
  • a play mode to open and to play recorded performances from saved files.
  • an step entry mode to permit accurate and detailed control over all aspects of a performance, control that is not possible to achieve in the run mode with improvised parameter changes.

FRACTAL MUSIC

Fractal Music generates images of the Mandelbrot set. Zoom into selected areas and click-drag rectangular sections of the images to be mapped to pitches. Most areas of the Mandelbrot set images do not produce interesting results, so it is wise to select small areas and continue to explore for areas that do produce interesting pitch patterns.



CELLULAR AUTOMATA MUSIC LIFE

The two dimensional cellular automata music application includes a game that can be played with the glider gun option selected.



CELLULAR AUTOMATA MUSIC 3D LIFE

A three dimensional implementation of 3D Life that has a seed function, but random seeding rarely results in sustained runs.

The user will usually select the Inject Life Forms option.



RandomlyBlue

RandomlyBlue uses probabilities to create pitch, percussion and transposition events. General MIDI percussion instruments can be added to instrumental ensembles such piano, bass and drum set.



SPHERICAL HARMONICS MAC

1983 "Spherical Harmonics" is Dale Millen's computer music project to digitally synthesize sounds of a vibrating gas filled sphere.

  • This project was assisted by members of the University of Arkansas Computer and Physics departments.
  • The computer program to calculate the frequencies of the vibrations was written in the Fortran programming language.

To get the frequencies of a hollow flexible sphere, one must choose the values of the following constants:

  • C1 = (density of membrane material) times (thickness of membrane)
              divided by (density of gas inside membrane) times (radius of sphere)
  • C2 = (tension of membrane) divided by (density of gas inside membrane)
              times (radius of sphere) times (velocity of sound in gas inside membrane) squared
  • C3 = 4 times (modulus of elasticity of membrane) times (thickness of membrane)
              divided by (density of gas inside membrane) times (radius of sphere)
              times (velocity of sound in gas inside membrane) squared

  • For each choice of n = 0, 2,3,4, ( n = 1 is excluded), the frequencies are given by
              frequency = x times (velocity of sound in gas inside membrane)
              divided by (2 times pi) times (radius of sphere)
              where the values of x are the solutions to spherical Bessel function equations for n = 0, n = 2,3,4.

The frequencies calculated with the Fortran program were entered into a Music 360 program to calculate digital sound files.
  • The digital computer tapes were sent to MIT for the digital-to-analog conversion
              and the converted analog sounds were returned as an analog reel-to-reel tape recording.
  • Music 360 ran on the University of Arkansas IBM 370 mainframe computer.
  • The Music 360 programming language was a descendant of the computer music languages
              originally created by Max Mathews at Bell Laboratories.
  • In the film, 2001: A Space Odyssey, Hal sings "Daisy" which is a reference to the digital synthesis of
              "Daisy Bell" made by Mathews in the early years of computer music (1962).

2021 Dale Millen created the "Spherical Harmonics" application in Swift programming language for Macintosh computer, based on the original 1983 Fortran version. Spherical Harmonics for macOS can calculate the frequencies and can instantly play the frequencies with dynamic envelopes.
